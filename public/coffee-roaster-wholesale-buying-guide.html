Evaluating Wholesale Suppliers

What to look for in a green coffee supplier

The five criteria that determine supplier quality — in priority order:

  1. Coffee quality and consistency. The product quality must match your needs and be reproducible lot to lot. Ask for multiple crop samples and compare. One excellent lot doesn't predict the next.
  2. Traceability and transparency. Can they tell you the farm name, region, altitude, variety, and harvest date? Specialty coffee requires this documentation for marketing and quality verification.
  3. Supply reliability. Can they commit to your volume at the same quality for 12 months? Specialty origins have seasonal supply — understanding harvest timelines and warehouse stock levels is critical.
  4. Commercial terms. Payment terms (30–60 days net is standard for established accounts), minimum order quantities, pricing lock availability, and logistics clarity.
  5. Relationship quality. Do they proactively communicate supply issues? Do they notify you when a lot you loved is available again? The best suppliers treat you like a partner, not a transaction.
Pricing

Understanding and negotiating green coffee pricing

Green coffee pricing has a floor (C market + differential) and a ceiling (what the market will pay). The negotiable range is the differential — the quality premium above the C price.

Leverage for better pricing:

  • Volume commitment. Committing to 5–10 bags vs 1–2 creates meaningful pricing leverage with most importers.
  • Payment speed. Early payment (before terms) sometimes earns a small discount. Ask.
  • Relationship maturity. Importers offer preferred pricing to long-term buyers with consistent purchasing history.
  • Competitive quotes. Getting quotes from 2–3 sources for the same origin creates pricing pressure. Don't pay 20% above market because you didn't comparison-shop.
Contracts and Terms

What to understand before signing

Green coffee purchase agreements typically specify:

  • Lot identification: Farm, region, processing, harvest year, grade/screen.
  • Volume: Number of bags and bag weight (60kg standard; 69kg for some origins).
  • Price: Per-pound or per-bag. May be fixed or pegged to C market + differential at time of shipment.
  • Quality acceptance: What defect or score standard the lot must meet for you to be obligated to accept.
  • Delivery terms: FOB (you take ownership at origin), CFR (cost and freight to destination), DDP (delivered duty paid to your door). Most US importer sales are delivered US warehouse.
  • Payment terms: Net 30, Net 60, or prepayment. New buyer terms are typically tighter.
Inventory Management

Managing your green coffee inventory

Green coffee inventory is a working capital decision as much as a sourcing decision. Principles:

  • Anchor 3–6 months of anchor origin supply. Supply disruption on your most-used lots is a business crisis. 3–6 months of buffer prevents it.
  • Don't over-buy specialty lots. Specialty single origins have shelf lives in consumer attention as well as physical quality. 1–3 months of supply is usually right for rotating featured origins.
  • FIFO strictly. First in, first out. Green coffee quality degrades over time. Last season's coffee shouldn't be sitting behind this season's.
  • Track moisture over time. A moisture meter on stored green is a cheap quality insurance policy. If moisture is rising (above 13%), investigate storage conditions.

Frequently Asked Questions

What should I look for in a coffee wholesale supplier?
Five criteria in priority order: (1) consistent coffee quality across lots, (2) traceability documentation (farm, region, variety, harvest), (3) supply reliability over 12 months, (4) commercial terms (payment terms, minimums, pricing lock), (5) relationship quality and proactive communication.
How do I negotiate green coffee pricing?
Key leverage points: (1) volume commitment — committing to 5–10 bags creates pricing leverage, (2) competitive quotes — get 3 comparable quotes before accepting a price, (3) payment speed — early payment sometimes earns small discounts, (4) relationship history — consistent buyers with clean payment records earn preferred pricing over time.
What are standard payment terms for green coffee?
Established accounts typically receive Net 30 or Net 60 payment terms (due 30 or 60 days from invoice). New buyers often start with prepayment or shorter terms (7–14 days). US importer sales are typically invoiced at time of warehouse release. Keeping a clean payment record is one of the most effective ways to build commercial relationship quality with importers.
How much green coffee inventory should I hold?
Anchor origins (most-used lots): 3–6 months of supply. Rotating specialty/single origins: 1–3 months. Exception lots (competition, micro-lots): buy what you can sell in 2–3 months — they're most valuable when fresh. Green coffee stored properly holds quality for 12–18 months, so carrying 6 months of anchor supply is financially and quality-safe.
What is DDP in coffee purchasing?
DDP (Delivered Duty Paid) means the seller bears all costs and risks to the buyer's location — including origin export, ocean freight, import duties, customs clearance, and delivery to your warehouse. Most US specialty importer sales to roasters operate as DDP US warehouse (or similar) — you receive a per-pound price that includes everything to your door.
How do I verify green coffee quality on arrival?
Inspect the shipment: verify bag count and condition, inspect for physical damage or pest evidence, check GrainPro bag integrity, measure moisture content (10–12% acceptable; above 13% is a quality risk). Document and photograph any issues. Notify your supplier within 24–48 hours of any quality discrepancies from contracted specifications.
What is a price lock in green coffee?
A price lock (also called a price commitment or forward contract) secures a specific price for a lot that will ship in the future. It protects against C market increases between contract and delivery. Most importers offer price locks on available lots; some offer forward contracts on upcoming harvests for trusted buyers. Useful for anchor origins where pricing volatility affects your product margin planning.
