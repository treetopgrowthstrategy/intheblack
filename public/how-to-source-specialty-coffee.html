How to source specialty coffee: the step-by-step process.

Sourcing specialty coffee well is a process, not an event. Here's the end-to-end workflow — from defining what you need through locking supply — that specialty roasters use to build consistent, high-quality green coffee programs.

Step 1

Define your needs

Before reaching out to any importer or broker, answer four questions in writing:

  • What origin or origins are you trying to source?
  • What profile are you targeting (bright/clean, full/chocolatey, process-forward)?
  • What score range do you need?
  • How much volume do you need, and over what period?

Written answers become your sourcing brief. Share it with every importer and broker you contact.

Step 2

Identify supply sources

For each origin on your target list, identify 2–3 potential supply sources:

  • Direct importers — call or email 3–4 importers who carry your target origin.
  • Brokers — for origins where your importer relationships are thin, a broker like In The Black Coffee Co. can open doors faster.
  • Online platforms — Algrano, Coffeehunt, and Sucafina offer direct sourcing from exporters.
Step 3

Request and receive samples

Contact each source with your sourcing brief and request cupping samples. Specify the lot details you're interested in (origin, processing, score range). Most importers will ship cupping samples (1kg) at no charge for commercial buyers.

Track: which source offered which lot, at what price, what volume is available, and the importer's own cupping score for comparison.

Step 4

Roast and cup

Roast samples light (full city, Agtron 60–65) within 24 hours before cupping. Rest 24 hours after roasting. Cup to SCA protocol. Score independently. Document: origin, processing, offered score, your score, tasting notes, physical green observations.

Cup blind where possible — remove lot labels until after scoring to prevent relationship bias.

Step 5

Evaluate and select

Compare your cupped samples against your sourcing brief. Prioritize: (1) cups that meet your score floor and profile target, (2) available volume that matches your needs, (3) pricing that fits your cost structure, (4) importer relationship quality for reorder reliability.

Step 6

Lock supply

Place your order. Be specific: lot name, volume (number of bags), expected delivery, payment terms. Confirm warehouse location and lead time. For anchor origins where you'll reorder, confirm whether this lot has additional volume and at what price.

Green coffee moves. Once you've identified the lot you want, lock it. Follow-up requests for the same lot two weeks later often find it's gone to another buyer.

Step 7

Receive, verify, store

Inspect upon receipt: verify bag count, inspect for damage, check bag seals (GrainPro bags should be intact), confirm moisture level against documentation. Store in a cool, dry environment (65°F or lower, 50–60% humidity). Palletized, off the floor, away from strong odors.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the coffee sourcing process?
Seven steps: (1) define your needs (origin, profile, volume), (2) identify supply sources (importers, brokers, platforms), (3) request and receive cupping samples, (4) roast and cup to SCA protocol, (5) evaluate against your sourcing brief, (6) lock supply with a purchase order, (7) receive, verify, and store the green coffee.
How long does it take to source specialty coffee?
From first outreach to green in your warehouse: typically 4–12 weeks. Sample procurement takes 1–2 weeks. Cupping, evaluation, and order placement takes 1–2 weeks. Ocean freight from origin takes 4–8 weeks. Air freight is possible for smaller orders in 1–2 weeks at premium cost. US warehouse orders from existing importer stock arrive in 1–5 days.
What is a cupping sample?
A 1kg (approximately) sample of green coffee sent by an importer or broker for a roaster to evaluate before purchasing. Most specialty importers offer cupping samples at no charge for commercial buyers who have an established or potential purchasing relationship.
How do I store green coffee?
Store green coffee in a cool (below 65°F), dry (50–60% humidity) environment. Keep bags off the floor on pallets. Maintain GrainPro bag integrity — reseal any opened bags. Avoid storage near strong odors (spices, chemicals) as green coffee absorbs aromatics. Properly stored specialty green holds quality for 12–18 months.
What do I look for when cupping green coffee?
Primary evaluation criteria: (1) fragrance/aroma — quality and intensity before water addition, (2) flavor — the complete taste impression after addition, (3) aftertaste — duration and quality, (4) acidity — intensity and quality (bright, citric, malic), (5) body — heaviness and texture, (6) balance — how all elements work together, (7) uniformity, clean cup, and sweetness. Score on SCA's 100-point protocol.
What makes a good green coffee lot for a specialty roastery?
In priority order: (1) a cup that meets your score floor and profile target (you'll serve this to your customers), (2) physical quality — low defects, good moisture, consistent screen size, (3) available volume that matches your needs without forcing over-buying, (4) pricing that allows your retail margin target, (5) importer reliability for reorder.
How do I find specialty coffee from Guatemala?
Contact specialty importers who carry Guatemalan lots (Cafe Imports, Royal Coffee, Atlas, Intelligentsia). Attend SCA Expo or World of Coffee to cup at importer tables. Or contact In The Black Coffee Co. — we represent El Quetzal Coffee Co. Guatemala and can provide direct access to SHG, Yellow Catuai, Geisha, Java, and Anaerobic Java lots.
Is direct trade better than buying through an importer?
Direct trade has a higher quality ceiling but requires significant relationship investment (farm visits, advance payment, custom contracts). Most roasters allocate 70–80% of green spend to importer relationships (reliability, credit terms, volume) and 10–20% to direct relationships (signature lots, high-ceiling quality). Neither is universally better — the right blend depends on your program size and resources.
