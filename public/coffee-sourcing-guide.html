Strategy First

Before you source: define your product strategy

Sourcing decisions are product decisions. Before evaluating a lot, answer:

  • Who are you buying for? Specialty enthusiasts who seek novelty? Espresso drinkers who want consistency? Cafe wholesale accounts that need 12 months of supply reliability?
  • What profile serves them best? Bright, clean, fruit-forward? Full body, low acidity, chocolatey? Adventurous, funky, process-forward?
  • What does your roasting program look like? Do you run 4 SKUs or 12? How often do you rotate single origins?
  • What's your green inventory capacity? Most small roasters can hold 3–6 months of green on hand. That determines order volume and frequency.

The answers create a sourcing brief — a document you share with importers and brokers that tells them exactly what you're looking for. Brokers with a clear brief source far better than brokers guessing what might interest you.

Origin Selection

Choosing origins for your program

Most specialty roasters run 3–6 origins at any time. A balanced program includes:

  • A workhorse Central American or South American washed lot. Reliable, versatile, 3–4 bag minimum available year-round. Guatemala, Honduras, or Colombia. Bright and clean with enough complexity for a featured single origin and enough consistency for espresso blending.
  • An Ethiopian for natural complexity. Yirgacheffe or Guji for the natural lots. High demand from specialty consumers; any specialty program without Ethiopian is leaving revenue on the table.
  • A process-forward lot for differentiation. A natural, honey, or anaerobic lot from any origin that demonstrates your sourcing curiosity. Conversation starters. High-margin retail coffees.
The Cupping Workflow

How to make buying decisions

A rigorous cupping workflow creates buying discipline and protects your quality floor:

  1. Request samples broadly. Get samples from 3–5 importers for any origin you're seriously considering. The competitive cupping table is the market.
  2. Blind where possible. Remove importer identity from the cupping table before scoring. Your job is to evaluate the coffee, not the relationship.
  3. Score to your standard, not theirs. Importers provide cupping scores. Treat these as reference, not gospel. Score independently and compare.
  4. Buy the coffee you'd serve to your best customer. Not the coffee that's available. Not the coffee with the best story. The coffee you're proud of.
  5. Commit volume when you find it. Good lots move. If you've found a coffee that works, lock supply. Don't hedge.
Building Importer Relationships

The relationship is the supply chain

In specialty coffee, the best lots go to the buyers that importers trust and value. That trust is built through: consistent payment, clear communication of your needs, specific and useful cupping feedback (not "I liked it"), buying when you say you're going to buy, and genuine interest in the stories behind the coffee.

An importer who knows you buy 10 bags of Guatemala annually, prefers bright and clean washed profiles, and always pays on time will call you before they call anyone else when an exceptional lot lands. That relationship is the real supply chain advantage.

Frequently Asked Questions

How do I source specialty coffee for my roastery?
Build 2–4 importer relationships covering your primary origins. Develop a sourcing brief that describes your profile preferences and volume needs. Request and cup samples from multiple importers before every purchasing decision. Attend trade show cupping events. For specific origins (Guatemala, Honduras, Ethiopia), work with brokers who have established relationships with quality-focused exporters.
What makes a good coffee sourcing strategy?
Three pillars: (1) clarity on your product brief — what profile, what application, what volume; (2) relationship depth with importers — the best lots flow to trusted buyers; (3) cupping discipline — buy on quality evaluation, not story or relationship alone. The roasters with the most consistent quality programs source systematically, not reactively.
How do I find quality green coffee importers?
SCA Expo and World of Coffee are the most efficient cupping table access. Most specialty importers maintain a cupping table at these events where you can taste their current offerings. Online platforms (Algrano, Coffeehunt) provide direct-from-origin access. Broker introductions (like In The Black Coffee Co.) provide warm access to specific origin supply with established relationships already in place.
What is a coffee sourcing trip?
An origin visit where a roaster travels to producing countries to meet farmers, cup green lots, and build direct relationships. Effective for roasters who want to develop direct trade relationships, verify the stories behind their coffees, and access micro-lots that aren't available through importer channels. Significant investment; appropriate for 10–15% of green spend.
How do I cup coffee to evaluate green purchases?
Request a cupping sample (1kg), roast light (full city, Agtron 60–65), rest for 24 hours. Cup to SCA protocol: 8.25g ground coffee per 150ml 93°C water, evaluate fragrance, aroma, flavor, aftertaste, acidity, body, balance, uniformity, clean cup, and sweetness. Score on 100-point scale. Compare to importer's offered score. Inspect physical green alongside the cup evaluation.
What is a coffee sourcing brief?
A written document summarizing your green coffee purchasing criteria: origin preferences, profile targets (bright/clean, full/chocolatey, process-forward), score range, volume requirements, seasonal availability needs, and budget. Sharing a sourcing brief with importers and brokers dramatically improves the quality of sample recommendations you receive.
How much coffee should I buy at once?
Most specialty roasters buy in 1–5 bag increments (60–300 lbs) per lot. Anchor origins that you run year-round justify larger orders (5–10+ bags) to lock pricing and supply. Featured single origins that rotate monthly warrant smaller orders (1–2 bags). Green coffee stored properly (cool, dry, sealed) holds quality for 12–18 months.
