Finding Green Coffee

Where to source green coffee

Specialty roasters buy green coffee through several channels, each with different tradeoffs:

Specialty importers (most roasters' primary channel)

Importers source, quality-control, warehouse, and offer credit terms on green coffee. Building relationships with 2–4 importers who carry your target origins gives you reliable access, priority on top lots, and a commercial partner who knows your quality requirements. Major US specialty importers: Cafe Imports, Falcon Coffees, Trabocca, Algrano, Royal Coffee, Atlas Coffee Importers, Mercanta.

Specialty brokers and agencies

Commission-based representatives like In The Black Coffee Co. who match roasters with specific origins or importer portfolios. Particularly useful for roasters trying new origins, accessing Central and South American lots, or working with origins where their importer relationships are thin.

Trade shows and cupping events

SCA Expo, World of Coffee, and regional cupping events let you cup from multiple importers in one place. Efficient for discovering new origins and building importer relationships. The importer cupping table is where most meaningful commercial relationships start.

Direct from farms and cooperatives

Appropriate for roasters who are ready to invest in a direct relationship (farm visits, custom contracts, advance payments). High commitment, high ceiling quality. Best for signature lots rather than volume anchors.

Evaluating Lots

How to cup and buy green coffee

Every green purchase should begin with a sample cup. The evaluation process:

  1. Request the sample. Most importers will send cupping samples at no charge for commercial buyers. Be specific about what you're looking for (processing method, score range, intended application).
  2. Roast light for cupping. A full city to light city roast (Agtron 60–65) preserves origin character. Roast the sample within 24 hours before cupping to let CO2 off-gas.
  3. Cup to SCA protocol. Grind at a medium-coarse setting, dose at ~8.25g per 150ml water (1:18 ratio), use 93°C water, crust at 4 minutes, evaluate fragrance, aroma, flavor, aftertaste, acidity, body, balance, uniformity, clean cup, and sweetness.
  4. Score against your standards. Document your score and notes. Compare against the importer's offered score — score inflation of 1–3 points is common.
  5. Evaluate physical green. Inspect the screen size, defect count, moisture content (request moisture data from the importer), and bean uniformity alongside the cup evaluation.
What to Ask Importers

Due diligence before you buy

Before committing to a green purchase, ask:

  • Where is the coffee warehoused? Know the warehouse location and expected lead time for your order to arrive.
  • What is the current moisture content? Should be 10–12%. Higher moisture risks mold and quality degradation in transit and storage.
  • What is the harvest year and crop date? Fresh crop (current or previous year) is best for most specialty applications. Old crop (2+ years) typically shows age-related flavor degradation.
  • What is the SCA defect count? Request the physical green analysis. Specialty grade requires zero category 1 defects.
  • What are the payment terms? Most importers offer 30–60 day terms for established accounts. New buyers typically start with prepayment or shorter terms.
  • Is there more available at the same price? Price-locked availability matters when you want to lock in supply for a season.

Frequently Asked Questions

Where can I buy green coffee for my roastery?
Primary channels: (1) specialty importers (Cafe Imports, Royal Coffee, Trabocca, Atlas) — your primary supply chain partners for anchor origins; (2) brokers and agencies like In The Black Coffee Co. for specific origins; (3) trade shows where importers present cupping tables; (4) direct farm and cooperative relationships for signature lots. Most roasters build 2–4 importer relationships and supplement through brokers and direct for specialized lots.
How do I evaluate green coffee quality?
Request a sample, roast light (Agtron 60–65, cupping roast), cup to SCA protocol (8.25g/150ml, 93°C, evaluate fragrance through sweetness), score on 100-point scale. Also evaluate physical green: screen size, defect count (specialty requires zero cat-1 defects), moisture content (10–12%), and bean uniformity. Compare your score to the importer's offered score.
What does green coffee cost?
In 2024–2026, specialty green coffee ranges from approximately $3.50–$8.00+/lb landed (US warehouse) depending on origin, score, and processing. Higher C prices in this period have elevated the baseline. Guatemalan SHG: roughly $3.50–$5.00/lb. Ethiopian naturals: $5.00–$8.00/lb. Geisha: $8.00–$20.00+/lb depending on score and farm.
What is SHG coffee?
SHG stands for Strictly Hard Bean (or Strictly High Grown) — a grade classification in Central American coffees (particularly Guatemala and Honduras) indicating coffee grown above 4,500 feet. At high altitude, beans develop more slowly, producing denser beans with higher acidity, complexity, and cupping potential. SHG is the premium grade; HB (Hard Bean) at 3,500–4,500 feet is the next tier.
What is anaerobic coffee?
Anaerobic processing is a specialty fermentation method where coffee cherries are sealed in oxygen-free tanks for a controlled fermentation period (typically 24–96 hours). The anaerobic environment produces distinct flavor compounds — often described as wine-like, fermented fruit, or funky complexity. Popular with adventurous specialty consumers; demanding and divisive in mass market. In The Black's Anaerobic Java from El Quetzal is a notable example.
What is a Geisha coffee variety?
Geisha (also Gesha) is an Ethiopian-origin arabica variety that produces exceptionally distinctive cup profiles — floral, bergamot, jasmine, and tea-like characteristics at high scores. First popularized by Hacienda La Esmeralda in Panama in 2004, Geisha is now grown in several Central and South American origins including Guatemala. Limited supply and exceptional cup quality command premium pricing.
How do I buy coffee directly from Guatemala?
Direct from Guatemalan farms involves establishing a relationship with a producer or exporter, negotiating terms, and handling logistics (export documentation, freight, import duties). For most specialty roasters, working through a broker or importer with established Guatemalan supply — like In The Black Coffee Co. representing El Quetzal — is more practical than building a direct relationship from scratch.
