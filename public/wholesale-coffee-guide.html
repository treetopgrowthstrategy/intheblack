The Foundation

How specialty wholesale coffee works

The wholesale coffee supply chain has four primary tiers:

  1. Origin/Farm. Coffee is grown, harvested, and processed. Quality is determined here — soil, altitude, variety, processing method, and the farmer's expertise create the potential in every bag.
  2. Export/Importer. Green coffee is exported from origin, typically through an in-country exporter, and imported by a specialty importer in the consuming country. The importer is your primary supply chain partner as a roaster.
  3. Broker/Agency. Intermediaries like In The Black Coffee Co. who represent specific origins or importer portfolios to roasters, often on a commission basis. Not all supply chains include this layer.
  4. Roaster. You. The buyer who transforms green coffee into a finished product for cafe and retail markets.

Most specialty roasters source through a combination of: direct importer relationships (for staple origins they run year-round), broker relationships (for smaller or harder-to-access origins), and occasional direct farm or exporter relationships (for signature lots or unique partnerships).

Pricing

How green coffee is priced

Green coffee pricing has two components:

  • C market baseline. The New York Coffee Exchange (ICE) "C price" is the commodity benchmark. It fluctuates daily based on global supply, climate forecasts, currency movements, and speculative positioning. In 2024–2025, the C price rose significantly due to climate-driven supply disruptions in Brazil and Vietnam.
  • Differential. Specialty coffee trades at a differential above the C price. Higher-scoring coffees, certified origins (organic, Rainforest Alliance, Fair Trade), and premium processing methods (washed, natural, honey, anaerobic) command higher differentials. A scoring 87+ lot from Guatemala might trade at C+150–C+200 cents/lb.

What this means for roasters: your green cost is the sum of C market + differential + freight + import duties + importer margin. Understanding each component lets you evaluate offers accurately and catch when you're being overcharged on differential for a commodity-grade lot dressed in specialty language.

Origin Guide

Key producing regions for specialty roasters

Guatemala

Guatemala produces some of the most consistently high-quality washed coffees in the world. Huehuetenango, Antigua, and Atitlán are the flagship regions, each with distinct terroir. Brightness, complexity, and stone fruit notes define Guatemalan specialty. El Quetzal Coffee Co. — a key supply partner for In The Black — sources premium Guatemalan lots including Yellow Catuai, Geisha, SHG, and anaerobic Java from verified farms.

Ethiopia

The birthplace of coffee. Yirgacheffe, Guji, and Sidama produce the most sought-after naturals and washed lots in specialty. Blueberry and jasmine notes in naturals; bergamot and lemon in washed. Among the highest-demand origins in specialty; supply is competitive, so importer relationships matter.

Colombia

Colombia's year-round harvesting (two seasons in many regions) and improving processing infrastructure make it a reliable high-volume origin. Huila, Nariño, and Cauca produce top specialty lots. The price premium over other Central American origins reflects its consistent market appeal.

Honduras

Often underestimated, Honduras produces excellent washed lots from Copán, Santa Bárbara, and Opalaca. Strong value proposition: 86–88 point coffees at differentials that undercut comparably scoring Guatemala and Colombian lots. A strategic anchor for cost-conscious specialty programs.

Kenya

The SL28 and SL34 varieties produce Kenya's signature black currant and wine-like profiles. Nyeri and Kirinyaga are the most prestigious regions. High demand globally; prices reflect it. Best suited for showcasing as a distinctive single origin rather than as a volume staple.

Quality Benchmarks

Evaluating green coffee quality

Specialty coffee quality is assessed on two axes: physical green and sensory (cup).

Physical green evaluation: screen size (larger is generally better), moisture content (10–12% optimal), defect count (SCA specialty grade requires zero category 1 defects and max 5 category 2), bean uniformity, and processing quality (presence of husks, chips, broken beans).

Sensory (cup) evaluation: SCA cupping protocol, scored on a 100-point scale across fragrance/aroma, flavor, aftertaste, acidity, body, balance, uniformity, clean cup, sweetness, and overall impression. 80+ = specialty grade. 85+ = excellent specialty. 90+ = exceptional.

Most importer offers include a cupping score. Verify by requesting green samples and cupping independently, or through a trusted third-party cupper. Score inflation is common in the industry; independent verification protects your product quality claims.

Building Your Supply Chain

The sourcing strategy that works

The most resilient specialty roaster supply chains have three layers:

  1. Anchor origins (60–70% of green spend). 2–3 origins you source year-over-year from the same importer relationship. Consistency, volume discounts, and priority access to top lots reward loyalty in the importer relationship.
  2. Rotating specialty lots (20–30% of green spend). Seasonally available micro-lots and cupped offerings that rotate through your featured single origin program. Bought on quality evaluation; not committed year-over-year.
  3. Opportunistic and direct (5–15% of green spend). Exceptional lots from competitions, direct farm relationships, or new importers building their book. Higher risk, higher ceiling quality, lower volume.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is wholesale coffee?
Wholesale coffee refers to coffee sold in bulk (typically green/unroasted or roasted in large quantities) to commercial buyers — roasters, cafes, food service, and retailers — rather than direct to consumers. Specialty wholesale coffee operates above commodity grade and typically includes traceability to specific farms, regions, and processing methods.
What is specialty coffee wholesale?
Specialty wholesale coffee is green or roasted coffee that meets specialty grade standards (SCA score of 80+ for green) and is sold with traceability documentation — origin, farm, variety, processing method, and harvest year. It commands a differential above the commodity C price that reflects quality, traceability, and often direct trade or fair trade practices.
How do I find wholesale coffee suppliers?
Primary channels: (1) Specialty importers — build direct relationships with importers who carry your target origins. (2) Brokers and agencies like In The Black Coffee Co. who represent specific importers or origins on a commission basis. (3) Trade shows — SCA Expo, World of Coffee, regional events where importers present cupping tables. (4) Direct farm partnerships — high investment, high ceiling, appropriate for 10–15% of green spend.
What is the C price for coffee?
The C price (New York Coffee Exchange, ICE futures) is the global benchmark for arabica commodity coffee pricing. Specialty green coffee trades at a differential above the C price based on quality, origin, certification, and processing. The C price fluctuates daily and is the baseline from which all green coffee pricing is calculated.
How much does green coffee cost wholesale?
In 2024–2026, green coffee pricing has been elevated due to C price increases (C traded at $2.50–$4.00+/lb in this period vs historical $1.00–$1.50). Specialty green ranges from roughly $3.50–$8.00+/lb landed depending on origin, quality score, and processing method. Micro-lots and competition winners can exceed $12–$20/lb.
What is a green coffee importer?
A green coffee importer sources green coffee from producing countries and makes it available to roasters in consuming markets. Importers maintain relationships with exporters and farms, handle logistics (shipping, customs, warehousing), provide quality documentation (cupping scores, processing notes, farm traceability), and typically offer credit terms to roasters.
What is direct trade coffee?
Direct trade refers to roasters buying directly from farms or cooperatives, bypassing traditional importer intermediaries. It typically involves personal farm relationships, higher prices paid to producers, farm visits, and a direct line of communication on quality and processing. True direct trade represents a small percentage of specialty coffee sourcing; most specialty coffee is sourced through importers.
